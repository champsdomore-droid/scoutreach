# Clief Notes Writing Style Guide

This is how we write in Clief Notes. Every lesson, every post, every resource follows these rules. The goal is clarity. If someone reads a lesson at 11pm after a long day, they should still be able to follow it and do something with it.

## Voice

Direct. Warm but not performative. We talk to members like smart adults who are busy and want to learn something real. We do not talk down. We do not hype. We show the work and let the results speak.

The community exists because people are tired of vague AI advice and tool tutorials that go stale in two weeks. We teach frameworks that hold up. That is the bar.

## Rules

**Short paragraphs.** Two to four sentences. Walls of text lose people on phones, which is where most members read.

**Simple words.** Use "use" instead of "utilize." Use "help" instead of "facilitate." Use "start" instead of "commence." If a shorter word says the same thing, use the shorter word.

**No em dashes.** Use commas, periods, or restructure the sentence. If you catch yourself reaching for an em dash, that sentence is probably too long anyway.

**No antithesis.** The "not X, Y" or "it doesn't X, it Y" pattern. AI writing defaults to this constantly. One per lesson is acceptable if it is genuinely the clearest way to say something. Two is pushing it. Three and the whole thing sounds scripted.

**No performed questions.** If nobody would actually pause and ask that question out loud, cut it. "But what does this mean for your workflow?" is performed. Nobody talks like that. Just tell them what it means.

**No hype language.** Never use "revolutionary," "game-changing," "groundbreaking," "cutting-edge," "industry-leading," or "unlock your potential." If the thing is good, describe what it does. The reader will decide if it is revolutionary.

**Bold key terms on first use.** When you introduce a concept or term the reader needs to know, bold it the first time. After that, normal text.

**Define jargon on the spot.** If you must use a technical term, explain it in the same sentence or the sentence right after. Do not assume the reader knows it. Do not link to a glossary and move on.

**Emoji markers for structure, not decoration.** We use specific emoji to mark sections (🎯, ▶️, 📝, 📥, 🔗, 💬, ⏭️). They help members scan the page and know where they are. We do not scatter emoji through body text for emphasis.

## Cross-links

Every lesson points to 2-3 other lessons. This makes the course feel like a web of connected ideas, not a textbook you read front to back. A member who finishes one lesson should always know where to go next based on what they care about.

Cross-link language should sound like a friend pointing you somewhere useful:

Good: "If you skipped the setup in 1.1, go do that first."
Good: "We go deeper into this in the Folder Architecture course (Level 2)."
Bad: "For further reading, see Lesson 3.2."
Bad: "Related content: [link] [link] [link] [link]"

Keep it to 2-4 links. More than that and people freeze.

## Check-in prompts

Every lesson has a check-in that asks members to DO something. Post their setup. Share what they built. Describe a problem they solved. The action is the learning.

Good: "Post a screenshot of your folder structure."
Good: "What workflow did you teach Claude? Describe the task, how many steps, and how long it used to take."
Bad: "What did you think?"
Bad: "Any questions?"

Those last two get silence every time.

## Paid vs free content

Free content teaches the idea. Paid content teaches the application. When you reference paid content from a free lesson, it should feel like a natural next step. Never a sales pitch. Never "upgrade now to access." Just "We cover implementation in [Course Name] (Premium)."

If a paid lesson could pass as a free lesson, it is not premium enough. Paid members are paying for depth, structure, and real-world application. Downloadable resources, production-grade templates, worked examples with real context.
