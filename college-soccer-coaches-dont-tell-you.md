# CHAPTER 1: THE RECRUITING GAME IS RIGGED — HERE'S HOW

## The Numbers Nobody Talks About

The NCAA says only about 7% of high school soccer players get recruited to play at the next level. That number sounds bleak. Discouraging, even.

But here's what nobody tells you: **Those 7% aren't always the most talented players.** They're the ones who understood how the system actually works — and acted on it.

Here's the reality: There are hundreds of college programs actively looking for players every single year. Division I, Division II, Division III. NAIA. Junior colleges. They're all recruiting. They all need bodies. And most of them are desperate.

The difference between getting recruited and getting overlooked isn't always talent — it's knowing the game and playing it wisely.

### Division I vs. Division II vs. Division III vs. NAIA vs. JUCO

Let me break this down in terms that actually matter to you:

**Division I**

- Schools: About 200 men's programs, 200 women's programs
- Full scholarships exist, but they're rarely "full" for individuals. A typical D1 roster has 25-30 players. The NCAA allows 9.9 scholarships for men's soccer, 14 for women's. Those get divided among the roster. A "full ride" basically doesn't exist in college soccer — what you get is a partial that covers some tuition, some room, some board.
- The competition is elite. These are the players who were standouts at the club level, often since they were 14 or 15.
- The timeline: Most D1 commits happen before senior year of high school. If you're a junior and haven't been contacted, it's very late.
- The reality: If your kid isn't getting noticed as a sophomore, D1 is statistically unlikely. That's not a knock on your athlete — it's just math.

**Division II**

- Schools: About 150 men's, 160 women's programs
- Partial scholarships are more common than in D1. The scholarship limits are similar (9.9 men, 14 women), but D2 schools tend to spread them around more.
- The competition is still good, but more accessible. Many D2 players could have played D1 but weren't getting recruited at that level.
- The timeline: Still recruiting in spring of senior year. Not as locked in as D1.
- The reality: A great option for regional-level players who want to compete at a high level without the D1 pressure.

**Division III**

- Schools: About 400+ programs for both men and women
- No athletic scholarships. Zero. This is important.
- BUT: Financial aid is often available based on academics or need. A D3 school with strong academic offerings can actually be cheaper than a D1 or D2 school with a small athletic grant.
- The competition varies widely. Some D3 schools are very good. Some are closer to club level.
- The timeline: Still actively recruiting in spring. Much more flexible than D1.
- The reality: This is the most realistic option for the majority of talented players who aren't getting D1 looks.

**NAIA (National Association of Intercollegiate Athletics)**

- Schools: About 250 programs
- Scholarships are available and often more flexible than NCAA schools.
- Competition varies — some NAIA schools are very strong, others are comparable to D2 or D3.
- The timeline: Recruiting continues through late spring.
- The reality: A legitimate option that's often overlooked. Many good players would thrive here.

**NJCAA (Junior College)**

- Schools: About 150 men's, 130 women's programs
- A pathway. Players can go to juco for 1-2 years, develop, then transfer to a 4-year program.
- More accessible. Many players use juco as a stepping stone.
- The timeline: Still recruiting late, sometimes even after the traditional seasons.
- The reality: A great option for players who need development or didn't get recruited initially.

### What Coaches Actually Want

Forget what you think coaches want. Here's what goes through their minds when they evaluate a player:

**Film (Highlights).** This is non-negotiable. Without video, your kid doesn't exist. Period. Coaches can't evaluate what they can't see. If you send an email without a video link, it goes in the trash. That's just reality.

**Athletic Ability.** Can this player play at this level? That's the first question. Not potential — current ability. What can they do right now?

**Positional Need.** What position do they play? What position does the team need? A great forward doesn't help a team that already has three great forwards. A solid defender who fills a need will get a look over a flashier player at a position that's already set.

**Character and Reliability.** This is huge. Coaches will tell you: "I'd rather have a slightly less talented player who's reliable and a great teammate than a 'star' who's a locker room problem." Soccer is a team sport. One bad apple can wreck a team chemistry.

**Academics.** Can your kid qualify? This matters more than people think. Coaches don't want to deal with ineligible players. And academic issues can disqualify you from eligibility even after you've been recruited.

**The secret most parents don't know:** D2, D3, and NAIA schools are desperate for good players. They don't have the recruiting budgets of D1. They rely heavily on players who reach out directly. That's your edge.

# CHAPTER 2: THE TIMELINE THAT MATTERS — AND WHY MOST PARENTS GET IT WRONG

## The Wrong Timeline

Most parents start thinking about recruiting in junior year. Some even wait until senior year. By then, they're already behind — and worse, they're stressed and scrambling.

The worst conversation I have is with a parent in October of their kid's senior year who says, "We need to figure out recruiting NOW." And I have to tell them that most of the opportunities have already passed for D1.

That's not being mean. That's being honest. And it's the conversation I want to help you avoid.

## The Right Timeline

Here's when things actually happen, step by step:

### Freshman Year: The Foundation

This is NOT about recruiting. This is about getting better.

**Your job this year:**

- Focus on improving. This is not the time to stress about college.
- Play on the best team you can find. High school is fine for most players. Club soccer is better if you can afford it and if your kid is serious about playing at the next level.
- Start building your athletic profile. Keep a document with games played, tournaments attended, notable achievements, goals, assists, anything that shows your growth.
- **Academics matter NOW.** Your freshman year grades count toward college requirements. Start building a strong GPA. This is non-negotiable.

**What coaches look at for freshmen:**
Honesty: They're probably not looking much at freshmen. But the habits you build now — training, grades, character — set the foundation for everything later.

**If your kid is behind:** That's okay. There's time. But you need to use this year to get serious about development.

### Sophomore Year: Getting Noticed

**This is when you START the recruiting conversation.**

- **Start reaching out.** Yes, now. Send emails to coaches at schools you're interested in. Even if it's just to introduce yourself and say "I'm a sophomore, I'm interested in your program, here's my Hudl link."
- **Create your highlight video.** Three to five minutes of your best clips. No music needed. Just show yourself playing in actual games — not practice, not highlight reels. Full-field clips that show your movement, positioning, decision-making.
- **Attend ID camps.** These are camps run by college coaches at their own campuses. They cost money — typically $50 to $200 — but this is where coaches find players. This is your best investment.
- **Get on a recruiting service.** Create a free profile on Hudl. Every college coach uses Hudl. It's the industry standard. Also check out SoccerWire, which connects players with coaches.
- **Start building relationships with your club coach.** They can make recommendations. They know coaches. They can be advocates for your kid.

**Pro tip:** Sophomore year is the sweet spot. Your kid is good enough to generate interest, but the pressure hasn't peaked yet. You've got time to build relationships.

**What coaches look at for sophomores:**
Now they're paying attention. They're looking at your sophomore year film to project what you'll be as a junior and senior. They're assessing potential.

### Junior Year: The Critical Year

**This is when most of the serious recruiting happens. No exaggeration: This is the most important year.**

- **January to March:** Contact coaches aggressively. Send your profile, your highlights, your academic info. This is the busy season for coaches looking at juniors.
- **April to July:** Go to camps, tournaments, and showcases. This is when D1 recruiting happens. If you're not at these events, you're not on coaches' radar.
- **Fall:** Junior season is your selling season. Be ready to share footage after EVERY game. Update your profile constantly.
- **Start narrowing down your list.** You should have 15 to 20 schools you're seriously interested in. Categorize them:
  - Reach schools (where you're a stretch)
  - Match schools (where you fit)
  - Safety schools (where you'd definitely get in)

**The reality:** Most D1 commitments happen before senior year. If you want to play D1, you need to be on coaches' radar by late junior year — ideally much earlier.

**What coaches look at for juniors:**
Everything. This is when they're making their lists. They need to fill roster spots. They're watching film, attending camps, making calls.

### Senior Year: Sealing the Deal

**Your job:**

- **August to October:** Final push for interest. If you haven't heard from coaches yet, send more emails. Visit schools if you can.
- **November to December:** Dead period for some contact, but use this time to make decisions. Narrow your list to your top choices.
- **January to April:** Signing period. This is when you commit and sign.
- **Don't stop playing.** Your senior season still matters. Keep training hard. Stay sharp.

**Important:** Even in your senior year, it's not too late. D2, D3, and NAIA schools are still recruiting in the spring. Don't give up just because it's February.

**What coaches look at for seniors:**
Final evaluation. Can this player help us now? Are they a good fit academically? Character check. Final yes or no.

# CHAPTER 3: THE MONEY TRUTH — WHAT NOBODY EXPLAINS ABOUT SCHOLARSHIPS

## The Scholarship Fantasy

Let's be brutally honest: Full scholarships in college soccer are basically a myth. They're not really a thing.

Here's why:

### Division I

The NCAA allows 9.9 scholarships for men's soccer, 14 for women's soccer. But a typical roster has 25-30 players. Do the math: That means the "full ride" doesn't exist. Even the best players typically get partials.

**What coaches will tell you:** "We can offer you X%."
**What that means:** You might get tuition covered, but room and board and books are on you. Or you get a small stipend.

### Division II

Same scholarship limits as D1 (9.9 men, 14 women), but the competition is less intense, so the offers tend to be slightly more generous. But still: partials, not full rides.

### Division III

**Zero athletic scholarships.** This is important. Do not expect any athletic money from a D3 school.

BUT — and this is a big but — many D3 schools have significant academic scholarships. If your kid has good grades, they can often get more money from academic aid than they would from an athletic grant at a D1 or D2 school.

### NAIA

Scholarships are available and more flexible than the NCAA. But again: partials are common.

### The Average D1 Player's Reality

**The average D1 player gets about 30% of costs covered through athletics.** The rest comes from grants, loans, work-study, and family contribution.

Let me say that again: Even with an athletic scholarship, you're probably still paying 70% of the cost. The scholarship covers some tuition. Maybe some room. That's it.

## What's More Important Than Full Scholarships

**Financial Aid.** Many parents don't know this, but D3 schools can actually be cheaper than D1 schools because of how their financial aid works. A D3 school might give your kid $25,000 in academic scholarships, making the total cost less than a D1 school that gave you a $10,000 athletic grant.

**Academic Scholarships.** If your athlete has good grades — 3.5 GPA or above — they can get significant merit aid. Often more than an athletic scholarship would provide. Many parents spend money trying to improve their kid's recruiting chances when they should be encouraging their kid to improve their grades.

**The Walk-On Path.** Many players earn roster spots as walk-ons (without scholarships) and earn scholarships later through performance. This is more common than people think. You don't need a scholarship offer to get on the team.

## The Conversation You NEED to Have

Before you spend $50,000 on a private school to "improve recruiting chances," stop and have this conversation:

**"Is this really going to move the needle?"**

Sometimes the answer is yes — if the school has a great soccer program and can get your kid seen by college coaches.

But often, the answer is no. That money is better spent on:

- Travel to better tournaments
- ID camps at colleges you're interested in
- Personal training
- Or just saved for college costs

The most valuable investment you can make in your kid's recruiting is your time and attention — not your credit card.

# CHAPTER 4: THE MISTAKES THAT KILL RECRUITMENT CHANCES

I've watched hundreds of kids get overlooked over the years. Here are the mistakes I see over and over:

## Mistake #1: Starting Too Late

The number one mistake parents make? Waiting until junior or senior year to start thinking about recruiting.

By then, the best players are already committed. The roster spots are filled. The coaches have moved on.

**Start the conversation in your sophomore year, at the latest.** The earlier you start, the more options you have.

## Mistake #2: Being Unrealistic

If your kid is a regional-level player — good but not elite — D1 is probably not in the cards. That's not being mean. That's being smart.

The best thing you can do is be honest about the level your kid can realistically play at, and find a program that fits. A player who dominates at the D2 level is going to be happier and more successful than a player who rides the bench at D1.

Being realistic isn't giving up. It's being smart.

## Mistake #3: Focusing Only on D1

D1 gets all the attention. It's on TV. It's what parents talk about. But there are hundreds of other programs that would love to have your athlete.

**D2, D3, and NAIA are legitimate options that are often a better fit.** Many D2 and D3 schools have excellent soccer programs. Many D2 and D3 players go on to play professionally. The path isn't just D1 or nothing.

## Mistake #4: Ignoring Academics

Coaches will tell you: "I'd rather recruit a player with a 3.5 GPA who's good at soccer than a 4.0 who's okay at soccer."

But here's the thing: **A player who can't qualify academically won't get recruited at all.** It's that simple. If your kid can't meet the academic requirements, all the talent in the world doesn't matter.

Keep those grades up. Every year matters. Starting in freshman year.

## Mistake #5: Trusting the Wrong People

The youth soccer world is FULL of people who want to take your money:

- Camp directors who promise more than they deliver
- "Recruiting services" that are basically scams
- Consultants who charge thousands and deliver little
- Club coaches who steer you toward programs that benefit them, not your kid

**Trust your gut.** If something feels off, walk away. If someone is pressuring you to spend money, that's a red flag.

The best recruiting happens through:

- Direct outreach from you to coaches
- ID camps where coaches see your kid in person
- Tournaments where coaches are scouting
- Recommendations from coaches who actually know your kid's game

You don't need to pay anyone to get your kid recruited. You need to get your kid seen.

## Mistake #6: Only Focusing on One Position

If your kid can play multiple positions, that's a selling point, not a problem. Coaches love versatility. A player who can play center back and right back is more valuable than someone who can only play one spot.

Don't limit your kid's recruitment by insisting they only play one position.

## Mistake #7: Bad Highlights

Your highlight video is your kid's entire recruiting presence. If it sucks, your kid won't get looked at.

Common highlight mistakes:

- Too long (keep it to 3-5 minutes)
- No context (who is this player? what position? what team?)
- Bad quality (can't see clearly)
- Only showing goals (coaches want to see all aspects of the game)
- No full-field clips (show games, not just highlights)

# CHAPTER 5: HOW TO GET NOTICED WITHOUT SPENDING A FORTUNE

Here's the truth: You don't need to spend thousands of dollars to get your kid recruited. Here's what actually works:

## The Basics (Free)

1. **Hudl.** This is non-negotiable. Get your kid's highlights on Hudl. Every college coach uses Hudl. It's the industry standard. Keep it updated.

2. **Email outreach.** Write personalized emails to coaches at schools you're interested in. Be specific about why you want THAT school. Don't send form letters. Show you've done your research.

3. **Social media.** Coaches are on Twitter, Instagram, TikTok. Share highlights. Tag coaches. Build an online presence.

## Low-Cost ($100-$500 per year)

1. **ID Camps.** This is the single best investment you can make. These are camps run by college coaches at their own campuses. You get seen by coaches in person. You get to see the campus. You get to ask questions. The ROI on ID camps is enormous.

2. **Tournaments.** Regional tournaments and national tournaments get heavy scouting. Play in the right tournaments and you'll get seen.

3. **Showcases.** Events specifically designed for recruiting. Do your research on which ones actually have college coaches attending.

## The Expensive Stuff (Often Not Worth It)

- **"Recruiting services" that promise too much.** Many are scams. They take your money and do little.
- **Elite camps costing $2,000+.** Unless your kid is already a top-level recruit, these won't help.
- **Private consulting.** Can help, but don't pay $5,000+ upfront. Get results before you pay more.

**What I'd tell every parent:** Invest in ID camps over anything else. That's where the coaches are.

## The Email That Actually Works

Most parents send emails that go straight to the trash. Here's how to write one that gets opened:

**Subject:** [Position] Player in [City] Looking to Continue Soccer at [Level]

> Dear Coach [Name],
>
> My name is [Player Name], and I'm a [Year] at [High School] playing for [Club Team]. I'm interested in learning more about [University]'s soccer program.
>
> I'm a [position] with [specific stats or achievements]. I've been watching your program and I'd love the opportunity to contribute.
>
> I've attached my highlights and would welcome the chance to discuss how I might fit into your team.
>
> Thank you for your time,
> [Player Name]
> [Phone] | [Email] | [Hudl Link]

**Keep it short. Be specific. Be humble. Don't attach too much — just the key info and a link.**

# CHAPTER 6: WHAT TO DO IF YOU'RE ALREADY BEHIND

Maybe you're reading this and your kid is already a junior or senior. Maybe you've already missed the "ideal" timeline. What do you do now?

First: Don't panic. It's not too late.

## If You're a Junior

- Email coaches NOW. Like, today. Introduce yourself.
- Sign up for ID camps this spring and summer.
- Play every tournament you can.
- Get your film on Hudl and keep it updated.
- Be realistic about your level, but also be confident in your abilities.
- Consider D2, D3, NAIA, and JUCO as options.

## If You're a Senior (Fall)

- It's getting late, but not too late.
- Email every coach at every school you're interested in.
- Ask about walk-on opportunities.
- Look at JUCO if you really want to play at the next level and D1/D2 isn't happening.

## If You're a Late Bloomer

Some kids don't develop early but bloom later. If that's your kid:

- Don't give up.
- Consider JUCO as a pathway.
- Keep playing. Keep training.
- Email coaches and explain your situation.

# CHAPTER 7: THE TRANSFER PORTAL REALITY

Here's something most parents don't know about: The transfer portal has changed everything.

Every year, thousands of players enter the transfer portal looking for new teams. This creates opportunities.

**What this means for you:**

- Even if your kid gets a roster spot, they might transfer later
- Roster spots can open up during the year
- The portal creates competition, but also opportunities

# CHAPTER 8: MULTI-SPORT VS. SPECIALIZING — WHAT'S BETTER?

Should your kid focus only on soccer, or play multiple sports?

**The case for multi-sport:**
- Prevents burnout
- Builds different athletic skills
- Colleges like multi-sport athletes
- Reduces injury risk from overuse

**The case for specializing:**
- More time to develop soccer skills
- More competition at higher levels
- Club soccer requires year-round commitment

**My take:** At the younger ages (under 14), multi-sport is better. As they get older, if they're serious about playing in college, they'll need to focus. But don't force specialization too early.
